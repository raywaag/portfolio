The Background

Clusterizr's mission is to ease and strengthen the local connection of UCF students & alum with local companies in the tech industry. On a team of UX engineers, designers, developers, and marketing specialists, Clusterizr came to life with a "by students, for students" motto. Leading the UX team to create progressive wireframes, models, sitemaps and prototypes was an amazing, productive experience.

My Roles

UX Researcher UX Designer Information Architect

Research

Clusterizr's inital proposall was a combination of Linkedin, Indeed, Ziprecruiter and UCF Handshake. But, of course, better. More responsive, better feedback, visually appealing, navigable. That said, before the design sprints, the team spent about a week and a half to conduct user analyses on current market sites. These included those listed above as well as JobCase, MyOpportunity, Glassdoor, Snagjob and Monster.

Current Clusterizr comparisons on market.

The Keepers

Components and features that made the analyzed sites successful
  • Asynchronous loading. As popularity of the AJAX, on-page loading is rising, we found it to be beneficial to job seeker sites as it increases user retention on where they are. The user shouldn't be stuggling to navigate Clusterizr. Our pages were never quite deep enough for breadcrumbs nor did we feel that would be an effective execution of information architecture.
  • Logged out job seaching. Some tested sites were inaccessible to the point where the user could not move until they creted an account or logged in. This is not a welcoming introduction of a company and we decided to make job searching (not job saving or applying) a task the user could whether they were logged in or not.
  • Simplicity. Finding a job is a hectic task in itself. Why would you want a messy environment to support that? As the unformation architects, we used only the space we needed to display data.

The Not So Great Angles

Market features that interfered with the experience
  • Responsiveness. Modern development should go without saying but responsiveness should always, always, always be factored into design. In some cases, a mobile-first approach is necessary as a site may contain a lot of data that needs to fit on a mobile interface.
  • Design diversity. UX Designers have their intention in the name - to enhance the user experience. A dull, repetitve design from page to page is not a way to enthuse the user nor does it invite them back. Using graphics, background shapes, gradients and font differentiations, the team created boards that were not too disconnected, but also brought variety to Clusterizr.
  • Navigation.There is innovative design and then there is just plain poor design. Some analyzed sites lacked navigational features very important to user interaction. It seemed as though it was an app modernization gone wrong. Clusterizr and all its glory needed proper naivagtion.
  • Overwhelming footers.Why a footer needs to be a novel is beyond me. Nesting the unnecessary routes that are in a lengthy footer can save space and appear less overwhelming to the viewer.

User Testing

The prototyped user persona is that of Kevin Gharam, a web programmer and UCF senior with the protfolio and experience to suit a great team. He lacks network connections in his area since he has been mainly focused on schooling. Clusterizr allows him to showcase his work, developer his profile, connect with companies and employers as well as stay updated on local inductry news.

Considering the User

For this application, there are two main user personas: the student and the recruiter.

Considering the students
  • Design. When brainstorming the overal design of this application it was important to look into the day to day interfaces that students are familiar with. These include Facebook, Instagram, Pinterest and Snapchat. What do these have in common? Modern simplicity.
    Facebook lended us a boilerplate to a simple, marketed interface while also obtaining many features. Clusterizr was able to materialize an interface that mastered white space and emphasized significant actions.
  • Message allowance. Even when designing for recruiters, we were still designing for students. A huge complaint from students and even current employees (in reference to LinkedIn) was the ample amount of unnecessary recruiting messages they received. For one, I can relate. But two, what could we do about this? Recruiters are just doing their job but at what cost? Not accomodating the fact that the recruiters position and student's interests are vastly different?
    It was a fair agreement that recruiters should be more informed about what their open position entails and the actual skills and interest of the student. But since we can't design for that, in our beta testing we are alloting an allowance of recruitment-specific messages. Recruiters are then able to post or privatley message about their job position. UX isn't just about design. It's abut human behavior and strategy.
Considering the recruiters
  • Searching. a large part of a recruiter's job is searching and finding. To accompanying this, we established a recruiter-centered search and result system that allowed them to narrow down candidates, find standouts in their field of recruitment and ensure that not only the process is efficient, but also the results were suitable.
  • Muscle Memory. Upon testing the Facebook interface with Clusterizr in mind, when marked all actions that were categorized as "muscle memory". Human Factors encompasses the user's mental memory of actions with technoological experiences. Without even knowing it, human's rely on these subconcisous gestures to complete technological tasks.
    In this bucket falls movements like scanning the top of the page for search bars, eyes jumping to the top right or the left-column of feeds to find their profile, familiar faces and advertisements on the right-most column etc. All sound familiar?
  • Who's got time for that? Students are busy. Students are lazy. And students really sppreciate their free time away from the books. So enters the question:

    how can we design this to remove hassle and annoyance to students who would rather be doing something else?

    Minimizing steps to set up profiles, find companies they love with filtering, connect with a click and navigate their messages seamlessly were a few of the answers we found to minimize the fear and hassle of, essentially, a 20-year-old's future career planning.
