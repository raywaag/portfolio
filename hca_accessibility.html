Accessibility

I wish this was advocated for more. I wish they taught this in classes to really nailed down its importance. In healthcare especially, accessibility means everything. It means an application that looks, works, navigates and process appropriately for everyone. From color to design to font to code, accessibility is in every stage of the UX process.

Back in my graphic design days, I had a client who was color blind. I hadn’t known it and was quite frustrated when he turned down all of my designs. With low contrast, the designs looked abnormal to him. And since I hadn’t even considered that when I was designing, issues arose in my deliverables. Long story short, he mentioned his color-blindness and we worked around that. But it shouldn’t have been his visual disability that mattered, it should’ve been the entire audience that could potentially be color-blind. That’s only one example of considering accessible products. Coded applications need to be used by a keyboard-only audience and screen readers. Colors need to have a high enough contrast so that everything is visible to users with poor vision or color-blindness. Designs should adhere to readability research - considering text length, character tracking and typeface.

In mobile and wearable products there is another layer of accessibility with its gestures and haptics. Components touch padding, gestures that don’t overlap, capabilities for blind users and then some.

Some more lesser known accessibility focuses are age, income level, education and geography. Accessibility is in everything and when implemented well, it can go unseen. But when a product is inaccessible, it neglects an entire sum of users. This is not only a sign of ignorance from a company but also can ignite lawsuits.

UX is about the users before anything else. Accessibility should be a priority.
