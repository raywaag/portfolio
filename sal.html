About

Save a Life Pet Rescue is an organization in Central Florida that rescues dogs, puppies and cats from high-kill shelters, giving them a second chance at life with a loving family.

Since their formation in 2006, they have successfully rehabilitated, spayed/neutered, and placed over 15,000 animals with warm, loving families!

Research

Current pet adoption org sites like The Shelter Project and The Humane Society were great market comparisons for the Save a Life site.
While the amount of content was not the same, interactions and sitemap hierarchy was better defined by running user tests on those sites.


Screen captures of Human Society Homepage and The Shelter Project pet details.





The Great

List of components and features that made the analyzed sites successful.

  • Simple naviagtion. Not an overwhelming amount of links and clearly titled.
  • Imagery. Specifically on the Humane Society's site, imagery plays a large role in capturing the emotion of the user. This is a technique often used by organizations that wish to completely convey their message and mission to the user.
  • Simplicity. The sites are rather simple and down to the point. A picture is worth a thousand words, so when images are used correctly, verbaige can take a wiser role.
  • Significant "DONATE" buttons. The Humane Society's site displays an emphasized donate button in the top navigation as well as through out the site. This gives the user ample opportunity to opt a donation.
  • "Give once or monthly" donations. Providing a simple option to give once or monthly when donating put the user in clear control.
  • Single page "details" sections. While analyzing the Shelter Project site, upon viewing the details of a specific dog, the extra text and images opens on the same page. This allows for a less extensive site map and quicker page load, giving the user the informaion they are looking for immediately without losing their place in the search results.

The Not So Great

List of problematic featues and elements that interfered with the user experience.

  • Too many directions. The Human Society's homepage takes a lot of directions. Movement and flow are necessary to navigate a user through a page, but the flow of information on that site page was uneven and sloppy. Consider the user path a hike from top to bottom...I would label this hike to be strenuous.
  • Color Theory. Another flaw that also adds to the lack of organization on the homepage of teh Human Society site is the overuse of colors. Creating a specific palette would help this case. Also, monochromatically color the social icons will give a more professional presentation rather than keeping them their actual colors.
  • Transition overlap. While asyncronous loading is a very great thing, it can also overlap current content depending on the event called. The Shelter Project runs into this issue when opening detail panels. There is a small timeframe where the panel is underneath the current content until the shift happens. Sometimes content can be loaded in a single space rather than pushing the current content down to make room.
  • Dated forms. The Humane Society has a very modern site design. It's neat and professional and we land on a footer with a dated looking form. This form could be updated with a simple deletion of strokes and mobile notations so it can better be contingent with the rest of the beautiful site.
  • Keep the site accessible at all times. There are a few instances on the Humane site where it directs the user to a PDF report in that same tab with no access back to the site except for the browser's back button. Relying on the browser to have tools that the site should have is a risk and the sitemap should reflect that. Opening the PDF in a new tab is a proper solution here.
  • Use text wisely. A paragraph of italicized, serif text is not the best of the eyes. Enough said.

Design

So, now that we have the do's and don'ts of our site and provided information from the client, our information architecture, sketching and wireframing can begin.

Firstly with multiple user flows in mind, I write out the sitemap and sketch board visions (these usually gain momentum in the virtual wireframing phase as seen below). Organzing the information on the site's pages in detail lets the client visualize the data and revise any information errors - ensuring all of the information is covered and in qulity order.

I then work on the home page and gather proper style guide elements from there including fonts (no more than 2 or 3 styles), images, the palette, button/input styles and measurments.

An Agile workflow is a must as it always reverts back to the main subject - the user. After each phase of the design and development process, user testing is run to allow for errors or issues to be resolved in an efficient manner. It also permits smooth transitions from the designers to the developers as they work together with changes and imporvements while perfecting the interface.

Inital sketches and wireframes



Board Architecture

  • Home: Summarizes Save a Life's misison prioritizing likely user intentions. For Save a Life, this meant emphasizing that week's rescues, addressing their mission, multiple opportunities to donate, a chance to get involved and also a contact form.
  • Adopt: Principally addressing the adoption event processes and location with the help of Google Maps then following to the current rescues with a filtering ability for puppies, dogs and cats.
  • About: Save a Life's mission once again, the team and an opportunity to get involved. This page will include asyncronous detail loading.
  • Get Involved: Simply, there are three general ways to get involved with the organizaiton. These ways will minimally be presented with single-page details when selected. There will also be a form at the foot of the page for position applications.
  • Contact: An immediate chance to contact Save a Life followed by FAQs to potentially provide answers to user's questions.
  • Donate: Donating will not have a page to itself, but will be a modal form that pops up whenever the "donate" button is selected anywhere throughout the site.


The Prototype

Once boards are created, user testing is run with the current interactions to check for errors, user time, retention rates and the enjoyment of the experience in whole. Changes are made accordingly via concurrent user tesing.
