Paws with a Purpose

About

In a group of 4, we had three hours of the Adobe Competition to brainstorm, strategize, design and prototype an application under the theme of "Veteran Connections". We ended up winning second place and walking out with quite an accomplishment in a 3-hour span. Additionally, all assets including the logo and photography were created by us.


Paws With A Purpose serves as a third party networking app to discover organizations that offer veterans service dogs with ease, simplicity and credibility.

My Roles

Strategist UX/UI Designer Information Architect Interaction Designer Presenter

The Challenge

The process of searching, discovering and reaching out to a service dog organization can be a complex, broad and uneasy journey. Paws With A Purpose is available to create a friendly, navigable environment for veterans to better understand and find organizations that are most suitable to them and their disabilities.

Paws With a Purpose is for approved veterans seeking their fur-ever companion. Age range varies with a majority above 35 years.

  • Extensive research of the nation's service dog organizations, their specialties and their selection processes.
  • Knowledge of veteran disabilities, they're relativity, commonality and the best options for those specific disabilities.
  • Multi-platform: iOS and android users.
  • Within the design experience, the veteran's disabilities need to be taken into account such as deafness and blindness.
  • PWAP is a visual experience. Images will be needed and retrieved from different organizations. Ray Waag Photography supplied the images for the PWAP application.

Research

(In the brief 3-hr time period we had)

Research was heavily invested in service dog organizations and their specifics to better customize PWAP. This allowed the team to develop pathways specialized to the user's disability type. Our biggest market competitor is NAGDU Guide & Service Dog Info. We were able to analyze what aspects of the app they did well and what the user reviews liked / disliked about their app. Other service dog applications were accused of being falsified, leaving us a great gap in the market.

A big issue NAGDU Guide & Service Dog Info faced was interface appearance. There was no graphical influence to their sitemap which allowed us a chance enhance our app in that area. Also, PWAP is an app specifically for veterans, therefore, our audience is very select allowing us to focus on catering to them and them only.

Key Pain Points

The current presence of veteran service-dog resources

  • Navigability was an issue with NAGDU Guide & Service Dog Info mainly because, the outside user is unaware of the app's depth. Discovering that there were only a few pages to the app was frustrating and deceiving.
  • This effected the usability of the app as well given that the information was not absorbed in an efficient fashion due to the user's now maddened state of mind.
  • No focus. Once graduated and ready to serve, service dogs are categorized into their best-fit roles. That is, hearing, guide, facility, etc. In our architecture, we knew we wanted a way for veterans to find a companion quicker. To do this, narrowing down their specific disability improves the intimacy of the product and provides a more efficient experience for our users.
  • The current market applications are information-heavy. They are appropriate for user's in search of laws and regulations regarding service dogs. Then again, you do not need an app for that.

Considering the Fundamentals

  • Time on task. continuous clicks, scrolls and questions can repel a user, leaving them ineffectively informed and leaving the app without users. To achieve an organized, timely application, PWAP consisted of clear and evident icons and buttons with the ability to reach the menu or go "back" at any time.
  • Error Rate. A seemingly obvious but easily overlooked component of the design and development is the rate of errors. It is vital for PWAP users to encounter a user experience wit correct paths, formatting and back-end ability.
    The design needs to promote effortless navigation. So that the user can answer where they are, where they came from and where they need to go next.
  • Retention Rate. this will also allow the user to recognize where they are within the app sitemap. Headers reminding the user where they are will ease their recognition of location in the app. For instance, if they select deafness as their disability, they'll meet a list of potential service dog organizations with a page header reminding them they selected deafness. Likewise, when they proceed and select an organization, they will be directed to the organization's PWAP "profile" in which the organization they are viewing will be evident.
  • User Enjoyment. a very important fundamental to the app's design is the user's enjoyment. Creating a visually appealing interface that includes infographics and imagery along with principal data will capture the user's amusement and compel them to continue the use of PWAP.

Reflection

This is a note of reflection on the application and improvements that could be made. Essentially, it's a UX audit on my own app.

There are a few things immediate hit my eye when reflecting on this application. A big one that I have since then come to care about immensely is accessibility.

Especially for an application serving users with physical, mental and situational disabilities, there are major flaws in some areas. Grant it, in the alloted timeframe I don't blame us for considering these issues but they should still be confronted and will be right now.

Good use of iconography
First of all, I applaud our then chaotic and stressed brains for considering the use of iconography with text. Icons-only can be misleading and text-only can be under-visual.

Bad consideration for color
It was a quick decision to not use red, white and blue as that palette is very clich√® in veteran material. But had we really looked into a color palette that met design system standards, we could've achieved greater contrast in key areas of the application.
There is a light blue-grey used throughout the application for captions, taglines and support text that is almost invisible. To a color-blind user or visitor with low-vision, this text would be useless.
The star ratings on organization profiles are also too light. Their contrast does not meet ADA Compliance and would need to be refactored.

Too little padding on touchpoints
Specifically, I found this issue on screens where the back arrow sits a bit too close to the hamburger menu. Being a mobile app, the user should have enough room to avoid mishaps. As a majority of veterans are men, these touchpoints are meeting larger fingers. This should have been considered.
Speaking of space, considering the application's "safe area" and bordering margins could've prevented some of the content running so close to the edge of the screen.

Centered nav links
This is less of an accessibility issue and more of a design enhancement, but I have come to appreciate the function of left-aligned data and lists. As the navigation menu is essentially a list, it would perform and appeal better if the text links were aligned left.

Close button?
When a hamburger menu opens, it is common for the user to expect a close button. In our application, the hamburger menu goes unchanged. Ideally, when the menu is activated that hamburger icon should shift into a close icon. Additionally, the words "menu" and "close" should be implemented here to inform the user of their capable actions.

Logout
In many scenarios of UX it's important to emphasize destructive actions, I first learned this when studying the iOS interface and have used this rule consistently in HCA Healthcare applications. That said, the "Logout" button within the navigation menu should be signified with color and a confirmation alert. That way users know the permanence of the action.

In Conclusion

All in all, I am really pleased with my team's ability to conjure an idea, strategize the workflow and engage appealing design in such a short time. This was my second Adobe UX Design Competition (we received second place in both competitions) and it is such a thrill to not only consider pleasing UI design but also a strategize that catches the judges eyes and workflow that actually signifies an excellent user experience.
